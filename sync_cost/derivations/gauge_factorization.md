# Direct-Product Structure of the Gauge Algebra from Klein Bottle Topology

## Theorem

Let P → K² be a principal G-bundle whose structure group G has a
Lie algebra g that acts on mode fibers compatibly with the Klein
bottle's topological coordinate decomposition. Then g admits a
direct-sum decomposition

    g = g_x ⊕ g_y,     [g_x, g_y] = 0,

where g_x and g_y are the structure subalgebras acting on the
antiperiodic (x) and periodic (y) coordinate directions respectively.
Simple Lie algebras, having no non-trivial direct-sum decomposition,
are excluded as candidates for g. In particular, su(6) — the unique
simple compact algebra whose center realizes Z₆ — does not arise as
the gauge algebra of the Klein bottle.

When Z(G) is identified with the framework's Z₂ × Z₃ residue structure
(`fiber_bundle.py`, `gap3_principal_bundle.py`), the minimum-rank
product with Z(g_x) ⊇ Z₂ and Z(g_y) ⊇ Z₃ is su(2) ⊕ su(3), giving
G = SU(2) × SU(3) (up to the U(1)_Y factor from the periodic direction).

## Setup

### The Klein bottle's two-coordinate structure

K² is the quotient of [0,1] × [0,1] under the identifications

    (x, 0) ~ (x, 1)             (y-direction periodic)
    (0, y) ~ (1, 1 − y)         (x-direction antiperiodic)

The two coordinate directions are topologically inequivalent: the
y-loop generates the free part of H₁(K²) = Z ⊕ Z₂, while the x-loop
generates the torsion part. No diffeomorphism of K² exchanges them,
and the divisibility assignment q ↔ temporal, p ↔ spatial
(`klein_bottle_derivation.md` Part II) distinguishes them further by
dynamical role.

### Mode fiber structure

A mode of the rational field equation at base point `(x, y) ∈ K²` is
a rational pair `(m_x, m_y) = (p_1/q_1, p_2/q_2)` with one rational
per coordinate direction. The XOR parity filter
`(q_1 + q_2) mod 2 = 1` selects denominator classes `(q_1, q_2) ∈
{(2, 3), (3, 2)}` (D19), giving the four irreducible modes {A, B,
C, D} of `gap3_principal_bundle.py`.

Scaled representatives of a mode are parametrized by an independent
pair of scaling factors `(k_1, k_2) ∈ Z × Z`, one per coordinate.
The GCD reduction

    (k_1 · p_1, k_1 · q_1, k_2 · p_2, k_2 · q_2)
        →  (p_1/q_1, p_2/q_2)

has fiber labelled by `(k_1 mod 2, k_2 mod 3) ∈ Z₂ × Z₃`. The
residue group acts componentwise:

    (a, b) · (k_1, k_2) = (k_1 + a mod 2, k_2 + b mod 3).

The Z₂ subgroup `{(a, 0)}` shifts only the first component; the Z₃
subgroup `{(0, b)}` shifts only the second. The two subgroups
commute and act on disjoint fiber coordinates.

## The topological factorization lemma

**Lemma.** The fiber coordinate `(k_1, k_2)` is canonically 2-factor:
the first factor tracks scalings of x-direction mode data, the second
tracks scalings of y-direction mode data. No Z₆-valued bijection on
the fiber identifies the two factors in a way preserved by the Klein
bottle's diffeomorphism group.

**Proof.** Any such bijection would realize the fiber as a cyclic
Z₆-torsor where the generator acts by shifting a unified label
`k = k_1 + 2 k_2 (mod 6)`. Under such a unified label, a Z₂
sub-action would combine x-shifts with y-shifts through the CRT
embedding Z₂ → Z₆ sending 1 ↦ 3, which corresponds to
`(k_1, k_2) → (k_1 + 1, k_2 + 0)` modulo (2, 3) only if k_2 is
left untouched — but the unified shift mixes them. Concretely,
generating Z₆ by `g = (1, 1)` under CRT gives the orbit

    g⁰ = (0, 0), g¹ = (1, 1), g² = (0, 2), g³ = (1, 0),
    g⁴ = (0, 1), g⁵ = (1, 2)

which mixes first- and second-component shifts at every step. This
orbit traverses the fiber by simultaneously changing both `k_1` and
`k_2`, i.e., by mixing x-scalings with y-scalings at each step.

A diffeomorphism of K² that respects the x/y topological distinction
cannot induce this mixing on the fiber, because the diffeomorphism
group of K² decomposes as Diff(x-loop) × Diff(y-loop) on the
generators of π₁, and each factor acts on its own scaling
coordinate. The unified Z₆ labeling is therefore not preserved by
the symmetries of the base. ∎

## Gauge-algebra decomposition

Let P → K² be a principal G-bundle with connection A. The connection
is a Lie(G)-valued 1-form on P; its pullback to K² decomposes by
the base's coordinate 1-forms as

    A = A_μ dx^μ = A_x dx + A_y dy.

**Compatibility condition.** The gauge action is compatible with the
Klein bottle's topological decomposition if the gauge transformations
generated by A_x preserve the fiber's first coordinate class and
those generated by A_y preserve the second. Equivalently: the
components of A along dx take values in a subalgebra g_x ⊆ g that
commutes with the components along dy, which take values in a
subalgebra g_y ⊆ g. The Jacobi identity forces

    [g_x, g_y] = 0,      g = g_x ⊕ g_y.

Without this compatibility, gauge transformations mix x-scalings
with y-scalings on the fiber, contradicting the factorization
lemma above.

**Theorem (direct-product Lie algebra).** Any gauge algebra
compatible with K²'s topological decomposition admits the direct-sum
form g = g_x ⊕ g_y with commuting subalgebras.

## Exclusion of simple algebras

**Corollary.** Simple Lie algebras are excluded as candidates for g.

**Proof.** A simple Lie algebra has no non-trivial ideal, hence no
decomposition as a direct sum of proper subalgebras. If g = g_x ⊕ g_y
with both g_x and g_y non-zero, then g is not simple. If one of
them is zero, the gauge action is trivial on one coordinate direction,
and no non-trivial gauge bosons propagate along that direction —
excluded by the Klein bottle's mode structure, which carries
non-trivial phase data on both coordinates. ∎

**Case su(6).** The algebra su(6) is simple, with center Z(SU(6)) =
Z₆. By the corollary, su(6) is not a candidate for the Klein bottle
gauge algebra, even though its center matches the abstract group
structure of Z₂ × Z₃.

This promotes the informal M3 argument of `gauge_sector_lovelock.md`
— that SU(6)'s cyclic center "does not admit this two-class
decomposition" — to a theorem: the obstruction is not merely the
absence of a natural decomposition in SU(6)'s fundamental
representation, but the absence of any direct-sum decomposition in
the Lie algebra itself.

## Minimum-rank selection within direct-product candidates

Among direct-sum algebras g = g_x ⊕ g_y compatible with the residue
structure:

1. **g_x center ⊇ Z₂.** Simple compact algebras with center Z₂ are
   su(2), so(2n+1), sp(2n), e₇, and higher-rank members of these
   families. Minimum rank is 1, achieved by su(2).

2. **g_y center ⊇ Z₃.** Simple compact algebras with center Z₃ are
   su(3) and e₆. Minimum rank is 2, achieved by su(3).

The minimum-rank product is su(2) ⊕ su(3). Higher-rank candidates
(so(5) ⊕ su(3), sp(4) ⊕ e₆, etc.) are not excluded by this
derivation alone; their exclusion relies on the additional
identification "one Klein-bottle denominator class per Cartan
generator per factor" (see `gauge_sector_lovelock.md` Part II,
criterion 1), which this theorem does not address.

With that rank identification accepted:

    g = su(2) ⊕ su(3)

giving G = SU(2) × SU(3) (up to discrete quotients). The U(1)_Y
factor attaches via the periodic direction and the
Gell-Mann-Nishijima relation (D43).

## Scope of the closure

**Closes**:

- The direct-product vs cyclic distinction at the level of the
  gauge algebra. Simple algebras with center Z₆ are excluded as a
  theorem, not as an informal two-class argument.
- The specific exclusion of SU(6) from the gauge-group candidate
  list.

**Does not close**:

- The existence of a continuous principal G-bundle with a
  Lie-algebra-valued connection over the Klein bottle's continuum
  descendant (the "Level A" step in `gauge_sector_lovelock.md`
  Premise 4; `gap3_principal_bundle.py` verifies only the finite
  Z₆-torsor). This derivation takes such a bundle as hypothesis.
- The rank of each direct-sum factor. The minimum-rank selection
  uses the additional identification "one denominator class per
  Cartan generator," which is not a consequence of the
  factorization theorem here.

The theorem therefore removes one identification from the
gauge-sector chain (the direct-product-vs-cyclic step) while two
others remain.
